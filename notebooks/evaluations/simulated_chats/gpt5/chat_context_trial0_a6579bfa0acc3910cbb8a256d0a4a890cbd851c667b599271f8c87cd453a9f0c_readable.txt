Guideline-framed overview (KRAS p.G12C–mutated metastatic NSCLC, previously treated)

- Position in guidelines
  - Major guidelines recognize KRAS G12C inhibitors as options for patients with advanced/metastatic NSCLC harboring KRAS p.G12C who have received prior systemic therapy (e.g., platinum-based chemotherapy and/or immunotherapy). Adagrasib is included among targeted options in this post-first-line setting, alongside sotorasib. Selection between agents may consider individual patient factors, toxicity profiles, drug–drug interactions, and logistical considerations. Guidelines emphasize molecular confirmation of KRAS p.G12C and appropriate clinical monitoring.

- Potential benefits (as framed by guidelines’ evidence summaries)
  - Tumor response and disease control: KRAS G12C inhibitors have demonstrated meaningful objective response rates and disease control in previously treated patients, offering an additional targeted line beyond chemo/immunotherapy.
  - Time-to-response: Responses typically occur within the first couple of cycles, which may be clinically helpful in symptomatic disease.
  - Intracranial activity: Adagrasib has reported activity in untreated, stable brain metastases cohorts, which is relevant when CNS involvement is present; guidelines note emerging data and encourage appropriate CNS imaging and multidisciplinary management.
  - Quality-of-life implications: Symptom improvement can occur with response, though formal QoL outcomes vary; toxicity management is important to maintain dose intensity where feasible.

- Potential risks and monitoring considerations
  - Common toxicities: gastrointestinal events (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea), fatigue, appetite decrease, and elevated liver enzymes.
  - Serious/clinically significant risks: hepatotoxicity (particularly with recent/ongoing immunotherapy exposure), QTc prolongation/arrhythmic risk, interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis (rare), and electrolyte abnormalities with dehydration.
  - Monitoring: guidelines emphasize periodic LFTs, ECG/QTc monitoring in at-risk patients or if symptoms arise, and vigilance for pulmonary symptoms suggestive of ILD/pneumonitis.
  - Drug–drug interactions: attention to CYP3A modulators, P-gp/BCRP substrates, and other agents with QT-prolonging potential; coordination with pharmacy and cardiology when relevant.
  - Special populations: consider hepatic/renal function status; review comorbidities (e.g., CAD) and concomitant medications (e.g., acid-reducing agents, antiarrhythmics).

- Prognostic context (non-directive)
  - In previously treated KRAS p.G12C–mutated NSCLC, targeted inhibition with a KRAS G12C inhibitor can extend progression-free intervals compared with no targeted options and may improve overall outcomes for a subset of patients. Median outcomes in trials provide a benchmark (MedicalResearch will detail), but individual prognosis varies with performance status, disease burden (including CNS), co-mutations, comorbidities, and tolerability.
  - Guidelines recommend ongoing assessment for clinical benefit and toxicity, with imaging at appropriate intervals and supportive care optimization.

Key clinical evidence for adagrasib (KRAZATI) in KRAS p.G12C–mutated NSCLC

- Principal studies
  - KRYSTAL-1 (phase 1/2): evaluated adagrasib monotherapy in previously treated KRAS p.G12C–mutated NSCLC; also included dedicated cohorts for CNS metastases.
  - Additional ongoing studies (e.g., KRYSTAL-12 comparing adagrasib vs docetaxel; combination trials with PD-1 inhibitors, EGFR inhibitors, SHP2 inhibitors) are expanding the evidence base.

- Efficacy outcomes (monotherapy in previously treated NSCLC; representative ranges from peer-reviewed reports and regulatory summaries)
  - Objective response rate (ORR): approximately 32–43% across reported cohorts.
  - Disease control rate (DCR): approximately 70–80%+.
  - Median time to response: about 1.4–1.5 months.
  - Median duration of response (DoR): roughly 8–9 months (with some responses exceeding 12 months).
  - Median progression-free survival (PFS): approximately 6–7.5 months.
  - Median overall survival (OS): approximately 12–14 months in mature datasets.
  - Intracranial activity: in a CNS-specific cohort of patients with untreated, stable brain metastases, intracranial ORR around one-third and intracranial disease control in the majority (e.g., IC-ORR ~30–35%, IC-DCR ~80–85%); some reductions in CNS lesion size and durability have been reported.

- Safety and tolerability
  - Common adverse events (any grade): nausea, diarrhea, vomiting, fatigue, decreased appetite, elevated AST/ALT, anemia; many are manageable with supportive care, dose holds, or reductions.
  - Grade 3/4 adverse events: occur in a substantial minority (on the order of 30–45% across reports).
  - Discontinuation due to AEs: generally in the single digits to low teens percentage.
  - Specific risks/monitoring:
    - Hepatotoxicity: transaminase elevations; monitor LFTs regularly, particularly in patients recently exposed to immune checkpoint inhibitors.
    - QTc prolongation: ECG monitoring may be warranted, especially in patients with cardiac comorbidities, electrolyte abnormalities, or concomitant QT-prolonging drugs.
    - Pneumonitis/ILD: rare but serious; evaluate new/worsening respiratory symptoms promptly.
    - GI toxicity: prophylaxis and early management of nausea/diarrhea can help maintain adherence.
  - Dose modifications: Prespecified algorithms for dose interruption/reduction are used for toxicity management in trials and labeling.

- Drug–drug interactions and precautions (consult full prescribing information)
  - Metabolism/transport: adagrasib is affected by CYP3A; caution with strong CYP3A inhibitors/inducers; it can also affect exposures of certain substrates (e.g., P-gp/BCRP).
  - QTc-prolonging agents: coadministration may increase risk; consider alternatives or monitoring.
  - Acid-reducing agents: check label for pH-dependent absorption considerations and manage accordingly.
  - Hepatic/renal impairment: follow label guidance; monitor closely if baseline hepatic function is abnormal.

- Interpreting impact on prognosis (non-directive context)
  - For previously treated patients with KRAS p.G12C–mutated NSCLC, adagrasib provides a targeted option with a meaningful chance of tumor shrinkage and median PFS in the mid–single-digit months, with some patients experiencing durable benefit.
